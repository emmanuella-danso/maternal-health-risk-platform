 Explainable Maternal Health Vulnerability Index (MHVI) for Ghana

 ABSTRACT

This project develops an Explainable Maternal Health Vulnerability Index (MHVI) for Ghana using regional-level maternal health and socioeconomic indicators. The index integrates antenatal care access, maternal health outcomes, and structural determinants such as education, wealth, sanitation, and healthcare access into a single composite score.

The MHVI identifies regional disparities in maternal health vulnerability, explains key drivers using interpretable methods, and provides a framework for policy prioritisation. Results reveal a clear north–south gradient in vulnerability, with northern regions exhibiting higher risk driven by poverty, limited healthcare access, lower education levels, and weaker infrastructure.

1. INTRODUCTION

Maternal health outcomes in Ghana vary significantly across regions due to differences in healthcare access, socioeconomic conditions, and environmental factors. These inequalities contribute to preventable maternal morbidity and mortality.

This project develops a Maternal Health Vulnerability Index (MHVI) to quantify and compare regional disparities in maternal health risk. The goal is to support data-driven decision-making for policymakers by identifying high-risk regions and the underlying drivers of vulnerability.

2. DATA

The dataset consists of 16 administrative regions of Ghana and includes the following categories of indicators:

Antenatal care access (ANC visits, timing, and tests)
Facility delivery and skilled birth attendance
Anaemia prevalence
Education levels
Household wealth status
Water and sanitation access

These indicators represent both healthcare system performance and structural determinants of health.

3. METHODOLOGY
3.1 Normalisation

All indicators were scaled to a 0–1 range to ensure comparability across variables with different units and distributions.

3.2 Direction Alignment

Indicators were transformed such that higher values consistently represent higher maternal vulnerability. Protective indicators (e.g., ANC attendance, facility delivery, sanitation access) were reversed prior to aggregation.

3.3 MHVI Construction

The MHVI was computed as an equal-weight composite index of all normalized and direction-aligned indicators.

Formula:

MHVI = average of all vulnerability-aligned indicators

3.4 Explainability Approach

Explainability was achieved by comparing regional indicator values against national averages to identify the strongest drivers of deviation contributing to vulnerability.

5. DISCUSSION

The results indicate that maternal health vulnerability in Ghana is primarily driven by structural socioeconomic and health system factors rather than isolated clinical indicators.

Regions with high poverty levels, lower education attainment, weaker sanitation systems, and limited access to skilled maternal healthcare services show significantly higher MHVI scores.

The observed spatial pattern highlights persistent regional inequalities in health system access and social determinants of health, particularly between northern and southern Ghana.

6. POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS

Based on the MHVI results, the following interventions are recommended:

Strengthen maternal healthcare infrastructure in northern regions
Increase deployment of skilled birth attendants in high-risk areas
Improve sanitation and water infrastructure in vulnerable regions
Expand maternal health education and awareness programs
Implement poverty-targeted maternal health support programs
Increase early antenatal care uptake through community outreach
7. CONCLUSION

The MHVI provides a transparent, interpretable, and data-driven framework for assessing maternal health vulnerability across Ghana. It enables policymakers to identify high-risk regions and prioritize interventions based on evidence rather than assumption.

This approach demonstrates how composite health indices can support national planning and reduce regional inequalities in maternal health outcomes.
